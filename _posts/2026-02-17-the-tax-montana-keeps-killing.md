---
title: The Tax Montana Keeps Killing
tag: Policy
lede: Montana has rejected a sales tax every time it's been asked. The economics guarantee the question will keep coming back. The politics guarantee it will keep losing.
---

In 1993, 74.9% of Montana voters rejected a statewide sales tax. The governor at the time, Marc Racicot, had barnstormed every corner of the state selling a 4% levy that promised to lower both property and income taxes. He had the state's largest teachers' union on board. He had the business lobby. He had bipartisan support in the legislature and a simple pitch: broaden the tax base, lighten the load on everyone. Montanans buried it by a margin of 49 points.

That wasn't the first time, and the political class hasn't stopped trying since. To understand why Montana remains one of five states without a general sales tax, you have to look past the bumper-sticker libertarianism that outsiders like to project onto this place. The real story is older, stranger, and more instructive than a simple preference for low taxes.

## The pattern

The cycle runs like clockwork, roughly once every decade or two. State revenues tighten. Property taxes climb. Some combination of governors, business groups, and well-meaning policy wonks floats a sales tax as the rational, modern solution. Voters crush it.

In 1971, facing a budget shortfall, the legislature put a 2% sales tax on the ballot as an alternative to an income tax surcharge. Pro-sales-tax forces were well organized and well funded. When opponents used a provision of Montana's 1912 Corrupt Practices Act to force open the campaign's books, the public learned that nearly every major corporation doing business in the state was bankrolling the effort. The information came to light on the eve of the vote. Montana voters rejected the proposal by a three-to-one margin.

Twenty-two years later, Racicot's version went down even harder. And in 1994, a coalition of conservatives who'd fought the '93 measure pushed through a constitutional amendment capping any future state sales tax at 4%. That amendment remains on the books today, a guardrail installed by people who didn't trust themselves — or, more accurately, their future legislators — to hold the line.

## What nobody told you

The debate has resurfaced with real urgency. Montana's most recent reappraisal cycle pushed up the median residential tax bill by 21%, following an average 46% increase in assessed property values in 2023. Governor Gianforte convened a bipartisan property tax task force in January 2024. The 2025 legislature passed two major reform bills, HB 231 and SB 542, that shifted some of the burden away from primary residences and onto second homes, short-term rentals, and commercial properties. For a median-value home — which is you, opening your tax bill in November and trying to figure out whether the number went up or down — the estimated savings top $1,000 per year.

But those reforms didn't settle the underlying question. They rearranged who pays, within a system that still balances on two legs: property taxes and income taxes. The Montana Chamber of Commerce president, Todd O'Hair, spent the summer of 2025 touring the state making the case that this two-legged stool can't hold much longer. A hypothetical 4% sales tax modeled by the nonpartisan Legislative Fiscal Division in 2024 estimated it could raise $1.3 billion a year, depending on exemptions. For context, $2.4 billion in property taxes were collected in Montana that same year. The sales tax wouldn't replace property taxes. It would give the stool a third leg.

And the polling? An MTFP-Eagleton poll published in early March 2026 found that only 33% of registered voters support a sales tax, even when framed as property tax relief. That opposition held across party lines: 34% of Republicans, 38% of Democrats, and 32% of independents voiced support. This isn't a partisan divide. It's a cultural consensus.

## The structural reason it keeps happening

Montana's tax system was designed for a resource extraction economy. Mines and timber mills owned enormous tracts of land, built expensive processing infrastructure, and paid property taxes proportional to all of it. When Anaconda Copper was the largest employer in the state, the math worked. Local governments could fund schools and roads and law enforcement on the industrial tax base without leaning too hard on individual homeowners.

Montana's economic structure has shifted toward services and tourism. The mines closed or scaled back. The mills went quiet. And the property tax burden migrated from industrial land onto residential parcels — which is to say, onto the people who actually live here. Montana gets 15 million tourists every year and has a little over one million residents. Those visitors eat at restaurants, sleep in hotels, buy gas, and use roads. They don't pay income taxes to the state, and outside of 11 resort communities that levy local option taxes of 3% to 4%, they don't pay sales taxes either.

The resort tax model is revealing. Whitefish voters approved their initial resort tax in 1996 and then, in 2015, voted to increase it from 2% to 3% with 84% support, largely to fund a conservation easement. Between 1996 and 2015, the resort tax brought in $27.5 million in revenue for the city. Big Sky levies 4%. These communities figured out, on a small scale, what the state refuses to accept on a large one: that visitors who use public infrastructure should contribute to maintaining it.

But Jeff Michael, director of research at the University of Montana's Bureau of Business and Economic Research, complicates the simple version of this argument. Montana's tourists don't spend as heavily on taxable retail as visitors in other states. They're hiking, driving, camping. They're buying gas, not designer handbags. Out-of-state addresses account for 33.8% of the taxable value of Montana residential property. In other words, nonresidents are already contributing to the property tax base by owning second homes. A sales tax that lowered property taxes wouldn't necessarily shift more burden to visitors; it might shift burden away from wealthy nonresident homeowners and onto lower-income Montanans buying groceries and boots.

This is the argument that kills the sales tax every time it comes up. It is, by the numbers, correct. A general sales tax is regressive. It takes a larger percentage of income from a family earning $45,000 than from one earning $450,000. The coalition that defeated the 1971 proposal — labor unions and the Democratic Party — understood this instinctively. The coalition that defeated it in 1993 — labor unions and far-right conservatives — understood it from opposite ideological directions and arrived at the same conclusion.

## The quiet part

Every sales tax proposal in Montana's history has arrived packaged as relief. Relief from property taxes. Relief from budget shortfalls. Relief from the structural imbalance of a two-legged revenue system. And every time, voters have looked at the package and asked the same question: relief for whom?

Bob Story, executive director of the Montana Taxpayers Association, put it plainly at the Bureau of Business and Economic Research's January 2026 seminar. When you ask Montanans about a sales tax directly, it's a hard no, he said. Start attaching conditions — lower your property taxes, cap the rate, dedicate the revenue — and you get close to a majority. Close, but still short. People are skeptical enough of government that they won't give a carte blanche.

That skepticism isn't irrational. Montana homeowners watched their property tax bills spike 21% in a single reappraisal cycle. They watched the legislature scramble to pass reforms that, by the state's own modeling, might save the median homeowner $1,000 a year but could raise bills on second homes and short-term rentals by as much as 68%. They watched the governor issue $400 rebate checks — a patch on a structural problem, funded out of a surplus that won't always be there. Every one of these moves reinforces the lesson that tax policy in Montana is reactive, not designed. Something breaks, someone tapes it together, and the conversation about the foundation gets pushed to the next session.

## What you can do about it

The sales tax debate is going to keep coming back. The economics guarantee it. Every year that Montana's tourism economy grows, the gap between who uses public services and who pays for them widens. Every reappraisal cycle that pushes residential property values higher tightens the vise on homeowners who haven't gotten a raise to match. The two-legged stool doesn't fix itself.

If you're a Montana voter, the useful question isn't "sales tax: yes or no." That question has been answered, repeatedly and emphatically. The useful question is what specific structural reform you'd accept instead, and whether your county commissioners, your state legislators, and your governor have an answer that goes beyond rebate checks and one-session patches. Ask them. Ask them what the revenue model looks like in 2035, when the tourism economy has grown another 20% and the mines haven't come back and the assessed value of your house has doubled again.

Because right now, the only people in Montana who've built a durable, voter-approved mechanism to make visitors pay for the roads they drive on are the 11 resort communities small enough to qualify for a local option tax, and the constitutional cap that limits any future sales tax to 4% was written by people who were trying to prevent one, not enable one.

Montana doesn't have a sales tax because Montanans have said no every time they were asked. What Montana also doesn't have is a plan for what happens when saying no stops being enough.
