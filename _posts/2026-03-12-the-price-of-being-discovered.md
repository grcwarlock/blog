title: The Vanishing Montana
tag: Housing
lede: Montana's housing crisis didn't start with the pandemic. It started with the zoning code. What happened next is a test case for the rest of the country.
---

In 2020, a typical home in Montana cost $296,000. By the end of 2025, that number had climbed to $460,700. For context, Montana's median household income is $72,509. That means the median home now costs roughly 6.4 years of gross household income — every dollar, before taxes, food, gas, or keeping the lights on. By one recent ranking, Montana is the third least affordable state in the country for homebuyers, measured strictly by the gap between what people earn and what houses cost.

The standard explanation for this is simple and partially true: the pandemic happened, remote work happened, and people with Seattle and San Francisco salaries started buying Bozeman and Whitefish real estate with cash. Between 2020 and 2021, nearly 20,000 more people moved into Montana than left, a surge that made the state the second-fastest growing in the nation. Those newcomers arrived with average household incomes of $110,000, compared to $82,000 for existing residents, and they bought homes at price points that pulled the entire market upward behind them. In Gallatin County, the inventory of unsold listings had essentially vanished. In Missoula, bidding wars pushed sales well above asking price. The math was brutal for anyone who already lived here.

## The ground was prepared long before

What tends to get left out of that story is how the ground was prepared long before any Californian loaded a U-Haul. Montana's housing shortage was a decade in the making, rooted in the same restrictive zoning and slow-growth permitting frameworks that choke supply in cities across the country. Before the pandemic, local jurisdictions had layered on single-family-only zoning, design review boards staffed by volunteers, minimum parking mandates, and subdivision processes that could delay a project by years. Each of these policies, taken individually, sounds reasonable. Stacked together, they form a system designed — intentionally or otherwise — to prevent the kind of incremental density that lets a housing market absorb population growth without prices going haywire.

This pattern repeats across the American West with remarkable consistency. Colorado's Front Range went through a version of it in the early 2010s. Boise saw it in 2018. Bend, Oregon. Coeur d'Alene. Asheville. The sequence is always the same: a desirable place with constrained housing supply gets "discovered," prices spike, longtime residents get priced out, and the political conversation oscillates between blaming newcomers and blaming developers without ever settling on the zoning code that made the squeeze inevitable. Montana followed the script almost perfectly, except for what happened next.

## The Montana Miracle

In 2023, the state legislature did something unusual. With bipartisan support and backing from Governor Gianforte's Housing Task Force, lawmakers passed what housing policy observers started calling the "Montana Miracle" — a package of zoning reforms that would have been unthinkable in most conservative legislatures. Senate Bill 323 required cities over 5,000 residents to allow duplexes in single-family zones. Senate Bill 528 guaranteed the right to build accessory dwelling units on single-family lots. Senate Bill 245 opened commercial zones in larger cities to multi-unit and mixed-use development. Senate Bill 382, the Montana Land Use Planning Act, overhauled local planning processes and required jurisdictions to reform their zoning to actually meet projected housing demand. When a homeowner group called Montanans Against Irresponsible Densification challenged the laws in court, the Montana Supreme Court upheld them unanimously, ruling that housing constitutes a legitimate state interest sufficient to override restrictive local rules.

Then in 2025, the legislature doubled down. Senate Bill 243 extended duplex requirements and eased height restrictions. Senate Bill 532 expanded ADU rules further, removing size caps from the original 2023 law. House Bill 492 capped parking mandates — no more than one space per unit for most residential construction, effective October 2026. New provisions addressed single-exit stairways, manufactured housing protections, and impact fee reform. The $225 million housing spending package that passed in 2023 included $105 million for water and sewer infrastructure, the kind of upstream investment that determines whether new housing is physically possible in a given area or permanently bottlenecked.

## Early signs

All of this is promising on paper, and some early signs suggest the reforms are working as intended. In Missoula, the median home price dipped 2.2% in 2025 to $550,000 while sales volume rose 4.6%, the second consecutive year of increased transactions after years of decline. The city's affordability index — which compares median income to monthly housing costs using the federal 30%-of-income threshold — improved to 82 for buyers with 20% down, the strongest score in years. Interest rates settled to around 6.3% by end of 2025, bringing a measure of predictability that had been missing. Supply has moved toward a healthier range, with roughly five months of inventory in the Missoula area by year-end.

But the structural problem persists at the lower end of the market, which is where the crisis actually lives for most Montanans. In Missoula, 22% of active listings sit at $1 million or above. Below the median price, inventory remains tight. Community land trust homes sold at a median of $257,028 in 2025, and when 27 new income-qualified units come online in 2026, demand will far outstrip availability. Two-bedroom rents climbed 11.4% in a single year, from $1,491 to $1,661. For a household earning the state median of $72,509, that rent consumes 27% of gross income on a two-bedroom alone, before utilities — and that's the median. For the 11.5% of Montanans living below the poverty line, or for the single-person households earning a median of $37,459, the math doesn't work at any price point currently available.

## The boom recedes

Meanwhile, the migration wave that caused the initial shock has receded on its own. Population growth fell to 0.52% in 2024, the lowest rate since 2002, and the first time in over a decade that Montana grew slower than the national average. Net domestic in-migration dropped from 20,500 in 2021 to 5,400 in 2024. Remote work, the engine of the boom, declined from roughly half the workforce at its pandemic peak to about a quarter by early 2025. Jeffrey Michael at the University of Montana's Bureau of Business and Economic Research put it plainly: Montana's growth will likely remain slow until housing prices and incomes become more balanced, and that rebalancing may take years. The problem, in other words, has begun to self-correct on the demand side, but the supply deficit that predated the boom still hasn't been filled.

## A test case for the country

What Montana is navigating right now is a test case for the rest of the country. A politically conservative state passed aggressive, supply-side zoning reforms with bipartisan votes. Those reforms survived a constitutional challenge. The legislature came back two years later and expanded them. The question is whether the reforms were enacted in time to matter for the people who need them most, or whether the affordable units they eventually produce will arrive after another generation of Montana workers has been pushed into longer commutes, doubled-up living situations, or neighboring states entirely.

---

If you live in Montana, the most useful thing you can do is find out whether your city or county has actually implemented the reforms the legislature passed. SB 382 required local jurisdictions to update their zoning to meet projected housing demand. SB 323 required duplexes in single-family zones in cities over 5,000. These are state law now, upheld by the state Supreme Court. Ask your city council or county commission whether they've complied. Ask whether they've approved any ADUs under SB 528. Ask how many new housing units have been permitted in the last twelve months compared to the twelve months before. The legislature gave local governments the tools. Whether those tools collect dust in a drawer is a local decision, made by people whose names are on a ballot you can actually reach.
